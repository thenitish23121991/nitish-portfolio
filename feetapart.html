Making people fitter with Feetapart
Feetapart(now furo.fit)
Aug 2019 - Nov 2019
I worked as the Lead Product Designer with Feetapart(on a contract basis) to redesign some of the key features of their app. I managed a team of 2 graphic designers, a UX researcher and an associate product designer. I was responsible for the overall UI/UX design changes on the app.

Apart from the design team, I worked closely with Product Managers(Tuneer Gaha) as well as the engineering team(Ashrith G(CTO), Jairus Fernandez(iOS Development Lead)).
About Feetapart
What Feetapart is and how it works
Feetapart is a gamification based health and wellness platform that offers exciting rewards to you for walking steps. For every step you walk, the pedometer turns your steps walked into a currency called Feetcoins, which can then be spent to redeem exciting rewards.
The Problem
Problem Statement
Feetapart was struggling with performance of some key metrics for their app including the store. They had some worrying data about user's engagement with their store, some of the users also told them they did not even know that they can redeem a product.
"How can we dispell the lack of trust users had in the Feetapart app and how can we improve the store experience and convince them to redeem more rewards?"
Looking at the Competitors
What other gamification apps were doing?
Flipkart and Myntra, two of the largest e-commerce websites in India already had a rewards store where the users could use the virtual currency they earned on the app to redeem rewards they might like. Target Users

INTERVIEWING USERS
Gathering User Insights
While the founders of Feetapart had already gathered some quantitative insights which was shared with me, we conducted a user research session to gather actionable qualitative insights to implement on. We gathered the following set of insights from our interviews with the users.
We interviewed 16 participants in the age group of 19-42 in Delhi and Bangalore. 11 of the participants were already fitness conscious while 5 of the participants chosen were looking to improve their daily fitness. Some of the findings of our interviews were:
  • Lack of trust:
    The users did not trust the gamification apps. They worried these apps were not genuine.
  • Too much work is needed to redeem rewards:
    Users did not realise that they could start getting rewards as soon as they would earn a few feetcoins on Feetapart. They did not need to walk for hours before redeeming rewards.
  • Looking for instant gratification
    The majority of our target users were millenials, they believe in instant gratification.
Testing the store with users
For gathering further insights on the store of the app, we gave a set of 5 users a task to be performed. The users would have to walk for a while to earn some feetcoins and then go to the app store and redeem rewards that they find exciting(or relevant to them).
  • The rewards that were shown to the users were not relevant to them, the rewards were shown in a simple list and appeared in the same order for every user. On the store screen,
    users saw an average of 21 products before checking a product out. For our competitors, it was an average of 7 products.
  • The users with very few feetcoins in their balance, they kept saying phrases like "I am not sure if I can redeem a product yet." "I dont have enough coins balance.". They did not realise that they already had enough feetcoins to redeem rewards.
  • We benchmarked our app against the Myntra app and observed that the
    users took ~11 seconds longer in getting to the store itself on our app as compared to the the Flipkart Store.
    This led us to hypothise that the navigation icons in the app home screen were not clear to the user. They shuffled between the icons in the navigation bar to get to the store.

Goals for the redesign
Our goals for the redesign were both improving general satisfaction of the users with the app as well to improve on some key metrics. I worked with Abhishek(Founder) and Tuneer(PM) to establish clear KPIs to measure success for our project:
  • Drop off rate from home screen - More people should visit the store.
  • Drop off rate from the store - More people should check out a product.
  • Users with less than 100 Feetcoins - Better product redemption rate.
  • Better product redemption rate in general.
  • Reduce time on task for visiting a product from the store.
Fixing app trust issues
Our assumption for the lack of users trust in the app was confusing design patterns along with unprofessional visual design. It was difficult to navigate through the app, along with lack of clarity in the overall visual design of the app. There were a few generic visual design issues throughout the app, the icons throughout the app were not cohesive, had different stroke widths and stroke radiuses throughout the app. We started by building a cohesive icons system with well defined characteristics to be followed for future icons. In the first iteration, we replaced the existing icons throughout the app with our new icons.
We found that the navigation bar was ambigious, especially for the newer users, hence we added corresponding text labels to the icons in the navigation bar.

Designing the Feetapart Store
Mocking up the approaches
We finalised on a couple of directions we can work further on, I mocked up both the approaches to have further discussion on. One of the approaches was to use a tabular structure with the available rewards and the redeemed rewards being the two tabs. The another approach was to use chips to show the most relevant products to the user straight away and allow a quick filter with chips to browse through the products that were close to them, unlocked for them etc. This is the approach we felt solved our problems in an elegent way.
Early Approaches
Designing the Feetapart Store
Settling on the Chips approach
We built on the Chips approach with further testing this users, we found that the gender of the user, how far away the product was from the user(location) and what products they could already redeem were the factors that made a product desirable for the users, hence we showed these filters as the easiest filters to be applied.

A/B Testing
A/B Testing options
We A/B tested 2 variations we had developed, with our users. One with the Feetcoins balance always present at the top, another one with the balance showing up once you scoll upwards(or back). Option A saw a better performance with:
  • Improved activity across a week in general for Option A.
  • Less drop off rate from the store.
  • Better product redemption rate.

    FEETAPART STORE ANIMATIONS
    Using animations to create a unified experience
    The animations used for the store are designed to be short, friendly and non jerky. The animations help outline the importance and heirarchy of elements of the store. The "New Arrivals" category is the stand out element on the store and hence is animated with the slowest timing function, so that the attention of the user is diverted to it.

    Measuring Impact and Further Improvements
    Feetapart launched the new store on 1st January, 2020 and based on the analytics we recorded for the first week as well as the messages received from power users, it was an instant success. The products redemption rate grew by ~900%, more than 16% of the total users checked a product out on the store. The change was also reflected in the rating scores users gave to the Feetapart app on the Google Play Store. The rating improved from a score of 3.3 to 4.2 within the first 3 weeks of launch. As a result of this impact, the Feetapart app trended on #3 in the Fitness Category on the Play Store for a week.
